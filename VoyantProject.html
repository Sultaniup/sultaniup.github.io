Analysis of Malcolm X’s Speeches in the 1960s

In this text visualization project, I plan to analyze Malcolm X’s speeches between 1960 and 1965. Malcolm X delivered more than 50 speeches at different places during the above-mentioned period in his defense of black Americans and their civil rights. The purpose of this analysis (with the help of “voyant”) is to probe in black rhetoric in the 1960s and see, in particular, how Malcolm’s belief in self-transformation determines the way he speaks and expresses his ideas. Here, I will do a visualization analysis on about 18 speeches delivered at different years, starting from 1960 to 1965.

According to the 18 speeches I have analyzed, the most frequent words are “white”, “black”, “man”, and “people” as I expected to find these words since Malcolm X talks about racial struggle in the country in the presence of hundreds of people.

In many speeches, as you see in the figure below, Malcolm X asserts the use of words like “Africa”, “negro”, “salve”, and “world” in some speeches. This, in my opinion confirms two critical notions. The first is that Malcolm X, like many other activists in the 1960s, still experience the legacy of slavery and act in resistance of new forms of enslavements. The second is that Malcolm X sees no hope in assimilation with the white majority in the country. He might aim for independence for African Americans by reminding them of their lost identity. The frequent use of these words makes us pay attention how history is important for African Americans if they want to achieve their civil rights. By making people cognizant of their history, Malcolm X attempts to close the “generation gap” that many young African Americans undergo in the 20th century. This attempts to connect the present with past is a way to increase people’s consciousness of their traditions and true identity.

Other words, which appeared in more recent speeches, like “Muhammad” and “God” denote a significant fact about his rhetoric in the 1960s: Malcolm X used religious rhetoric to create a sense of nation instead of community. Malcolm X also refers to “Elijah Muhammad” in the majority of the speeches, which reflect the name of a significant leader in the nation of Islam in the United States. I can say that Malcolm X believes in the power of religion to make the nation he dreams of in order to achieve freedom for all black Americans and their communities. The “revolution” that appears frequently in his speeches is a desire to have a sepatate black community, which many balck activists call “Black Nationalism.”

In the figure below we see that Malcolm X no longer uses religious discourse in some speeches of the 1964 and all of the 1965 speeches because he separated from the Nation of Islam after disagreement with the leader of the nation Elijah Muhammad. In fact he changed his religious rhetoric with a new discourse of civil rights after it was announced that he plans to meet with Martin Luther King immediately after he left the Nation of Islam.

Malcolm X also emphasizes the importance of consciousness for black community. He frequently uses the words “know” and “think” to support his direction towards a nation whose members are fully cognizant of their rights and identity.
